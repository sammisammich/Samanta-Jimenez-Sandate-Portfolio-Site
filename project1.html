Overview

Who is Culturally Relevant Science?

Culturally Relevant Science (CRSci) is a nonprofit dedicated to creating innovative, affordable, and inclusive K–12 STEM curriculum — especially for students from underrepresented communities. They believe every student deserves to see themselves reflected in STEM education.

Outcome: Improved curriculum accessibility for teachers and introduced structured analytics for admins, reducing friction in both content discovery and performance tracking.

The Problem

A Google Drive system wasn't cutting it

All curriculum materials were stored in a large, unstructured Google Drive — creating significant friction for both teachers and administrators.

Secondary Research

The case for more inclusive STEM curriculum

Before speaking to users, we grounded ourselves in the broader landscape. The data made the stakes clear.

35%Women in the STEM workforce, despite being 51% of the US population
9%Black workers in STEM, compared to 11% of the total US workforce
6.7%Scientists of color featured in biology textbooks, though 35% of biology students are people of color
1 in 7Women highlighted per seven male scientists in intro biology textbooks

Research by NYU identified four core reasons Black and Latinx students lack persistence in STEM: significant knowledge gaps on entry, lack of early STEM exposure, existing knowledge not utilised in classrooms, and a weak connection between their lived experiences and STEM content. CRSci directly addresses all four.

User Research

Listening to teachers and administrators

We conducted interviews with K–12 science teachers and school administrators who use CRSci in their classrooms. The sessions surfaced consistent patterns around what's working, what's frustrating, and what educators need most from a digital hub.

What teachers loved

"It's not boring… kids are automatically excited. They see CRSci as fun time because the content is so engaging."

"If you are any type of content-based teacher, you know you have to adapt content to fit your audience. This gives teachers that autonomy."

"I like how engaging and relatable the material is to all children. Representation really influences how students connect with the content."

"It's essentially a scripted lesson plan. Everything is provided — content, videos, subject-specific lessons — and it highlights representation."

Key pain points

⚠

Can't edit or copy slides. The content is view-only and locked, preventing teachers from fixing errors or adapting for their students.

⚠

Google Drive ↔ Microsoft friction. Most school districts run on Microsoft, forcing teachers to log into personal Gmail accounts just to access CRSci materials.

⚠

No update notifications. Teachers only discovered new content by logging in and noticing a new unit had appeared — no alerts, no emails.

⚠

No feedback channel. Teachers found errors in presentations but had no way to report them or suggest improvements to the CRSci team.

What educators wanted in a new hub

  • Central location for materials, clearly separated by folder with short, readable names
  • Standards embedded and easy to align to lessons
  • Pronunciation tool for science vocabulary to support students
  • A feedback/comment section for teachers to communicate directly with CRSci
  • Walkthrough video for new teachers — the platform should be self-explanatory
  • All units available and ready to go from the first day of the school year
  • Admin dashboard showing teacher engagement, log-in frequency, and progress data

Design Decisions

Choices grounded in user needs

  • Structured curriculum library: Organised by grade and unit to reduce search time, mirroring the mental model teachers already use in Google Drive
  • Preview-first interaction: Lets teachers quickly evaluate lessons before downloading, reducing wasted effort
  • Role-based dashboards: Separate, tailored experiences for teachers and admins so neither role sees irrelevant information
  • Simplified analytics: Clear visual summaries with at-a-glance stats instead of dense data tables that admins found overwhelming
  • Standards embedded: Learning objectives and state standards surfaced directly within each lesson, addressing the #1 non-negotiable educators cited

Usability Testing

What testing revealed

We ran two rounds of usability testing with real teachers and administrators, including video-based remote sessions. Participants completed four tasks: finding a lesson, downloading materials, managing teachers, and browsing new courses.

"Clean-cut, user-friendly. The specifications by standard and the download file make it easy. But learning targets on the platform that teachers can copy would be really valuable." — Mr. Javarius Powell

What worked well

Tasks 1 & 2 — Navigation
5 of 6 participants found the first two tasks clear and easy to complete. The lesson preview and download flow were praised consistently. Participants liked having lesson history and downloads visible in the sidebar.
Overall impression
Participants described the interface as "intuitive," "user-friendly," and noted even a substitute teacher would be able to figure it out. The separation of notes, worksheets, and slides was consistently praised.
Dual navigation
Users appreciated having two navigation points (top bar and sidebar). One participant noted: "I liked how there were two linkable navigation points" — this reduced dead ends significantly.
Standards visibility
Multiple participants requested learning objectives and standards to be surfaced on the daily section view — this was the single most repeated feature request across all sessions.

What needed fixing

Tasks 3 & 4 — Back navigation
5 of 6 participants had trouble going back and forth between screens. The fix: adding a clear close button and back button throughout the flow.
Subtopic hyperlinking
When module sections were expanded, 2 participants expected subtopics to be clickable links. They weren't — a simple fix that significantly improved task completion for Task 4.
Confusing wording
One participant couldn't complete Task 1 due to unclear wording on a button. Another hit every button except the highlighted one. Copy clarity became a priority revision.
Browse courses placement
Participants expected "Browse Courses" higher in the navigation hierarchy. Downloads were also expected to appear below the inbox rather than in the current position.

Reflection

What I took away

This project strengthened my ability to design for real-world systems where organisation, clarity, and efficiency are critical. Working with a nonprofit client introduced constraints that required balancing user needs with practical implementation — and reminded me that the stakes of inclusive design are genuinely high when the end users are students who have historically been underserved.

I learned that improving user experience isn't always about adding features, it's often about restructuring information and simplifying workflows. The fact that teachers were finding workarounds (logging into personal Gmail, converting files manually) told me everything about where the system had failed them.

Improving UX isn't always about adding, it's often about restructuring and subtracting until the right thing is obvious.
