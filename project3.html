Overview

Turning "I don't know what to do" into an adventure

ROAM is a mobile app that transforms local exploration into guided, gamified experiences. Instead of scrolling endlessly through Yelp or TikTok, users join curated scavenger hunts, discover hidden local gems, earn rewards, and share their adventures with friends.

The Problem

Young adults are stuck in a loop

Young adults want to explore their cities but face real decision paralysis. The result? They default to the same routines rather than discovering what's around them.

Research

33 survey responses, clear patterns

About 60% of respondents use Google Maps, Yelp, TikTok, or Instagram to discover activities — but none of these provide guided, interactive outings. Discovery is fragmented, choices are overwhelming, and the social dimension is missing.

Users jump between multiple platforms to plan a single outing, creating friction and drop-off before they ever leave the house.

Survey findings — discovery behaviour and pain points across 33 respondents

The three big insights

  • Discovery is fragmented: Users jump between platforms to plan one outing, causing drop-off
  • Too many choices: Overwhelm leads to repetitive behaviour and missed opportunities
  • Social experiences drive action: Users are significantly more likely to try new things with others

My Role

Interaction design, validation — and advocacy

Usability Testing Research Synthesis Friction Mapping Navigation Design Wireframing Hi-Fi Prototyping

I led usability testing and synthesis, identified friction points in early flows, and drove iteration on navigation and the roam creation experience. But beyond execution, I was also an advocate for features the team was hesitant to pursue.

Two features I pushed for specifically: the points and rewards system, and GPS map functionality. Both were initially flagged as too complex to implement. My argument was simple — apps like McDonald's and Yelp have proven that users engage deeply with location-based rewards when the UX is clear. The complexity isn't a reason not to build it; it's a reason to design it carefully. Both features made it into the final prototype.

Wireframes

From rough to refined

Low- and mid-fidelity wireframes let us explore scavenger hunt flows, map progress tracking, and social invitation patterns before committing to visual design. This is where we caught our most expensive mistakes early.

Low-fidelity wireframes — exploring core flows before visual design

Three issues surfaced immediately. Feature discoverability was low — users were missing key actions entirely because nothing drew their eye to them. The roam creation flow was confusing; people didn't understand what they were building or why. And the navigation had dead ends that forced users to restart flows they'd already started. Each of these pointed to the same root cause: we had designed around our own mental model of the app rather than how a new user would actually encounter it.

Branding & Design System

An identity that feels like an adventure — and a lesson in color

The design system was built around warmth, exploration, and play — using a mascot and badge system to reinforce the gamified experience throughout. But getting here wasn't straightforward.

Our first usability tests revealed a problem we hadn't anticipated: users were struggling to navigate quickly because the button colors weren't communicating hierarchy clearly. Actions that should have felt obvious were getting missed. The warm palette we'd chosen felt right aesthetically, but we hadn't been rigorous enough about contrast and visual weight between primary, secondary, and inactive states. We went back and rebuilt the color system with usability as the primary constraint rather than aesthetics, and task completion times improved noticeably in the next round of testing.

Hard Decisions & What's Next

The features we wrestled with

One of the most debated features was a group roam mode — the ability for friends to complete a hunt together in real time. The idea was compelling and our research clearly showed that social motivation drives exploration. But we couldn't responsibly design it without addressing real safety concerns: how do you let strangers join a roam without creating risk? How do you handle location sharing between users who don't know each other well? We didn't have good answers, so we didn't ship it. It's the right call, but it's one I'd want to revisit with proper safety research behind it.

The features we'd prioritise in a next version: tiered rewards and streak bonuses, co-op team hunts with proper privacy controls, multi-day roam planning, and weekly community challenges.

Reflection

What I'd do differently

The thing I'd go back and change most is how late we figured out the point system. We knew from the start that rewards were core to the concept — that's what makes ROAM gamified rather than just another discovery app. But we kept deferring the hard questions: what do points actually get you? How do you earn them in a way that feels fair? How do you display them without cluttering the UI? By the time we tackled it seriously, we were already deep into visual design, which meant the points system felt bolted on rather than woven in.

If I started again, the reward architecture would be one of the first things I'd define — not the last. That constraint would have shaped better decisions earlier in the process.

The point system should have been a design constraint from day one, not a feature we figured out at the end. That's the kind of lesson you only learn by getting it wrong.
